Zone Setpoint Profile (All Models)
Model 1 — Maximum Setpoint
Setpoint Formula
SP = max(Recipe₁, Recipe₂, ..., Recipeₙ)
What it does: Sets ALL 8 zone temperatures based on the HIGHEST recipe temperature among all slabs currently in the furnace.

Zone setpoints: Each zone = max_recipe + zone_offset
(Z3 offset +77°C, Z4 +71°C, Z5–Z7 +15–18°C, Z1/Z8 +6–8°C)

When correct: New grades being trialled, API/quality-critical campaigns, FCE dropout frequently below recipe, high spread (>20°C).
✓ Zero underheating risk
✓ Simple, robust
✗ Higher fuel cost
✗ Overheats low-T grades
Model 2 — Mass-Weighted Average
Setpoint Formula
SP = Σ(Wᵢ × Rᵢ) / ΣWᵢ + buffer
W = slab mass, R = recipe temp
What it does: Weights each slab's recipe temperature by its mass. Heavier slabs (more thermal inertia) influence the setpoint more.

Buffer rule: +5°C safety margin added. If any slab recipe > wtavg+10°C, that grade gets a zone-specific override.

When correct: Stable mixed production, known grade behaviour, spread 5–15°C, energy-saving campaigns.
✓ ~0.9% fuel saving
✓ Adaptive to mix
⚠ Moderate quality risk
✗ Needs tighter control
Model 3 — Residence-Time Weighted
Setpoint Formula
SP = Σ(RTᵢ × Rᵢ) / ΣRTᵢ + buffer
RT = residence time, R = recipe temp
What it does: Weights by how long each slab spends in the furnace. Slabs with longer residence time (colder start, heavier mass) need more heating → their recipe gets more weight.

Why better for Hazira: Data shows ANTRG55 (low recipe, 1220°C) has avg RT=168 min vs ANGENR2 (high recipe, 1240°C) at 165 min — similar, so M2 ≈ M3 here. But in schedules where cold slabs stay longer, M3 is more physically accurate.

When correct: Mixed cold-charging/hot-charging schedules, wide RT variance (>40 min spread).
✓ Physically meaningful
≈ Similar to M2 for Hazira
⚠ Needs RT data real-time
⚠ Why Zone 8 Temperature Can Be Lower Than FCE Dropout — This Is Correct Physics
The apparent paradox: Z8 (exit zone) actual mean = 1,237°C, FCE dropout mean = 1,238°C. Z8 appears cooler than what comes out.

The explanation: The slab arrives at Z8 already heated to ~1,310–1,320°C from soaking zones Z3/Z4. By the time it exits through Z8, it is releasing heat to the cooler atmosphere, not gaining it. The FCE pyrometer reads the slab surface temperature just before discharge — which is the equilibrium between retained soaking-zone heat and Z8's equalising atmosphere.

Z8 is an equalising zone, not a heating zone. Its purpose is to homogenise top-to-bottom temperature differences, not add bulk heat. The zone atmosphere can be slightly below slab temp for this to work.
